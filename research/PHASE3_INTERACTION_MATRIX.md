# Phase 3: Sequential Interaction Rules

## The Core Question

How do products applied at step N affect products applied at step N+1, N+2, etc.?

---

## Interaction Type 1: Silicone → Subsequent Product Deposition

### Does amodimethicone from conditioner block later products?

**Finding: NO — amodimethicone does not meaningfully block subsequent product deposition.**

Evidence:
- Amodimethicone forms a thin, self-limiting film (per Lab Muffin/Wong 2024). The film is not thick enough to create a barrier.
- The film is hydrophobic but not impermeable. Water-based products (leave-ins, gels) can still deposit on top.
- Per Cosmetics & Toiletries (Dow Silicones, 2022): amino-modified silicones form a "thin, hydrophobic layer" that protects but does not occlude.
- Practitioner consensus (curly hair community): silicone conditioners followed by gel is a standard, well-functioning routine. No evidence of gel cast failure from amodimethicone.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** Using L'Oréal EverPure conditioner (amodimethicone) before L'Oréal 21-in-1 leave-in (also amodimethicone) is fine. The self-limiting property means the second product won't over-deposit — it will fill any remaining damaged sites not covered by the first.

### Does dimethiconol (Dove conditioners) block subsequent products?

**Finding: POSSIBLY — heavier non-selective silicones create a thicker coating that may reduce subsequent deposition.**

Evidence:
- Dimethiconol forms a thicker, more uniform coating than amodimethicone (per HairAide 2025: "thick, protective coating").
- Non-selective deposition means it coats healthy AND damaged areas equally.
- A thicker silicone film could reduce the negative charge available for cationic products (leave-ins, gels) to bind to.
- However, this is a matter of degree, not a binary block. The effect is "reduced efficiency" not "complete prevention."

**Practical rule for Mandy:** When using Dove conditioner (dimethiconol), subsequent leave-in products may deposit slightly less efficiently. This is another reason to prefer amodimethicone conditioners (L'Oréal, Garnier) — they leave more binding sites available for later steps.

## Interaction Type 2: Leave-In → Gel Cast Formation

### Does silicone-containing leave-in interfere with gel cast?

**Finding: DEPENDS ON AMOUNT — light application is fine, heavy application can interfere.**

Evidence:
- The gel cast forms when styling polymers (PQ-69, VP/VA copolymer) dry and crosslink into a rigid film around hair clumps.
- A thin layer of silicone (from leave-in) provides a smooth base that the gel polymer can still adhere to — the polymer's cationic charge attracts it to the hair surface regardless.
- However, if the leave-in creates a very thick, slippery coating, the gel may not form as strong a cast because the polymer film has less "grip."
- Curly hair community consensus: leave-in BEFORE gel is the standard LCEG (Leave-in, Cream, Enhancer, Gel) method. It works. The key is not over-applying the leave-in.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** Apply L'Oréal 21-in-1 leave-in spray lightly (2-3 sprays per section, not drenched) before gel application. The spray format naturally limits over-application compared to a cream leave-in.

## Interaction Type 3: Lamellar Rinse (Wonder Water) → Conditioner/Leave-In

### Does Wonder Water affect subsequent product deposition?

**Finding: ENHANCES subsequent deposition, does not block it.**

Evidence:
- Wonder Water deposits cationic surfactants (cetrimonium/behentrimonium chloride) that smooth the cuticle.
- A smoother cuticle surface actually allows more uniform distribution of subsequent products.
- L'Oréal's own instructions: "Follow with your normal conditioning routine" — designed to be used BEFORE conditioner or leave-in.
- The cationic surfactant layer is thin (monolayer) and does not create a barrier.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** Wonder Water at step 4 (after conditioner rinse, as a final rinse) OR before conditioner both work. Current routine uses it as final rinse — this is fine. The smooth surface it creates helps the leave-in distribute more evenly.

## Interaction Type 4: Pre-Shampoo Protein → Conditioner Absorption

### Does protein pre-treatment affect conditioner deposition?

**Finding: MINIMAL EFFECT — protein fills internal gaps, conditioner coats the surface. Different targets.**

Evidence:
- Hydrolyzed proteins (low/mid MW) penetrate INTO the cortex (per Malinauskyte et al. 2020).
- Conditioner ingredients (silicones, cationic surfactants) deposit ON the cuticle surface.
- These are non-competing mechanisms — one works inside, the other outside.
- The protein treatment is rinsed out by shampoo before conditioner is applied, so there's no direct interaction between the two products on the hair simultaneously.
- Any high-MW protein that remains on the surface after shampooing would be minimal and wouldn't meaningfully block conditioner deposition.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** OGX Bond Protein pre-shampoo → shampoo → conditioner is the correct order and there's no interference. The protein does its work inside the cortex; the conditioner does its work on the surface.

## Interaction Type 5: Olaplex → Everything Else

### Does Olaplex 3 interact with subsequent products?

**Finding: NO INTERFERENCE — Olaplex works at the molecular bond level, completely independent of surface treatments.**

Evidence:
- Bis-aminopropyl diglycol dimaleate works INSIDE the cortex, reconnecting disulfide bonds between keratin chains.
- It does not deposit on the surface, does not form a film, does not change the surface charge.
- It is fully rinsed out by shampoo — only the reconnected bonds remain.
- Olaplex's own guidance: use as pre-shampoo, then proceed with normal routine.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** Olaplex 3 can be used interchangeably with OGX protein pre-shampoo in the step 1 slot. Neither affects subsequent steps.

## Interaction Type 7: Volatile Silicones → Nothing

### Do OGX oils (cyclomethicone-based) affect subsequent products?

**Finding: NO LASTING INTERACTION — volatile silicones evaporate.**

Evidence:
- Cyclomethicone (cyclopentasiloxane) evaporates within minutes of application.
- Once evaporated, only the small amount of non-volatile oil (coconut, argan) remains.
- This residual oil is too thin to meaningfully block subsequent products.

**Practical rule for Mandy:** OGX oils can be used at any point without worrying about interactions. They're essentially inert from an interaction standpoint (which is also why they provide minimal lasting benefit).

## Key Takeaway for the App

**There are essentially NO problematic interactions in Mandy's current routine.** The products are compatible and can be layered in the recommended order without concern. The only interaction to watch for is:

1. Don't over-apply leave-in before gel (when gel is purchased)
2. Prefer amodimethicone conditioners over Dove dimethiconol conditioners for maximum subsequent product efficacy

This is good news for the recommendation engine — it means product selection can focus on FUNCTION (which role does this step need to fill?) rather than worrying about complex interaction rules.
