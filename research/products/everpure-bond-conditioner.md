## 1. Executive Summary

The EverPure Bond Repair Conditioner is a well-constructed conditioning formula built on the industry-standard cetearyl alcohol + behentrimonium chloride base, with amodimethicone as the primary silicone (position 4 — high concentration). The current official formulation (May 2026) is notably streamlined — only 21 ingredients — with no dimethicone, no non-selective silicones, and no concerning preservatives. It uses citric acid for pH maintenance and arginine for protein support. This is an excellent primary conditioner for damaged, coarse, curly hair: the amodimethicone selectively conditions damaged areas, behentrimonium chloride provides exceptional detangling, and the leave-on contact time (1-2 minutes recommended) allows meaningful deposition.

**Verdict:** Tier confirmed — Primary Rotation. No change needed.

## 3. Mechanism Assessment

### 3A. Cetearyl Alcohol — Structural Base

**Position:** 2 (very high concentration — this is the body of the conditioner)

Cetearyl alcohol is a fatty alcohol (mix of cetyl and stearyl alcohol) that forms the structural matrix of the conditioner. It creates a lamellar gel network with behentrimonium chloride — layers of fatty alcohol and cationic surfactant that deposit onto hair during rinsing.

**Function in this formula:**
- Provides slip and lubrication during application
- Forms the vehicle that carries amodimethicone to the hair surface
- Contributes to cuticle smoothing (fills gaps between lifted cuticle scales)
- Creates the creamy texture that allows even distribution through thick hair

**Assessment:** Standard and effective. Position 2 means high concentration — appropriate for a conditioner targeting coarse, thick hair that needs substantial lubrication.

### 3B. Behentrimonium Chloride — Primary Conditioning Agent

**Position:** 3 (high concentration)

**Verified:** Per HairAide (2025): "Behentrimonium Chloride is a long-chain cationic (positively charged) quaternary ammonium compound derived from rapeseed oil. It is one of the most effective conditioning and detangling agents used in hair care, particularly prized for its ability to smooth the cuticle and dramatically improve combability." Its C22 carbon chain provides "exceptional slip, softness, and detangling." Best for: "All hair types, curly hair (3A-4C), tangled hair, damaged hair, color-treated hair, coarse hair." ([HairAide, 2025](https://hairaide.com/ingredients/behentrimonium-chloride/))

**Verified:** Per La Pink (2025): "Structurally, it features a behenyl (C22) long-chain alkyl group attached to a trimethylammonium head, making it a cationic surfactant. The nitrogen carries a positive charge, balanced by a chloride anion." ([La Pink, 2025](https://beauty.lapink.com/blogs/ingredients/behentrimonium))

**Mechanism:** The positive charge on the nitrogen is attracted to the negatively charged hair surface (especially damaged areas where the protective F-layer has been stripped). The long C22 alkyl chain then lies flat along the hair shaft, creating a lubricating layer that reduces friction between strands. This is the same charge-attraction mechanism as amodimethicone, but behentrimonium chloride is water-soluble and washes out more readily — it provides immediate conditioning that partially persists between washes.

**Assessment:** Behentrimonium chloride at position 3 is a premium choice. It's the gold standard for detangling coarse, curly hair. The C22 chain length is specifically chosen for thick/coarse hair — shorter-chain quats (like cetrimonium chloride, C16) provide less slip. For Mandy's thick, coarse 2C-3A hair that tangles easily when wet, this is the correct primary conditioning agent.

### 3C. Amodimethicone — Selective Silicone Conditioning

**Position:** 4 (HIGH concentration for a silicone in a conditioner)

This is the same amodimethicone discussed in the shampoo deep dive, but here it's at much higher concentration and in a leave-on-longer format (1-2 minutes contact time vs. seconds in a shampoo rinse).

**Delivery system:** Trideceth-6 (position 9) + Cetrimonium Chloride (position 15) form the emulsion delivery system for the amodimethicone. This is the standard amodimethicone emulsion package.

**Verified:** Per Lab Muffin (Wong, April 2024): Amodimethicone selectively binds to damaged areas via protonated amine groups attracted to negatively charged damaged hair. It self-limits via like-charge repulsion — won't build up layer upon layer. ([Lab Muffin, April 2024](https://labmuffin.com/amodimethicone-my-new-favourite-hair-ingredient/))

**Delivery format validation:** In a conditioner with 1-2 minutes contact time, amodimethicone has significantly more opportunity to deposit than in a shampoo. The cationic emulsion breaks during dilution (rinsing), releasing the silicone to deposit on the hair surface. The longer contact time allows more complete deposition, particularly on the most damaged areas.

**Key advantage over dimethicone/dimethiconol:**
- Selective deposition → more where needed, less where not
- Self-limiting → no progressive buildup requiring clarifying
- Lighter feel → doesn't weigh down curls
- Compatible with the established decision: "Amodimethicone conditioner every wash"

**Assessment:** Amodimethicone at position 4 in a conditioner is the ideal delivery scenario for this ingredient. High concentration + adequate contact time + selective deposition = maximum benefit for damaged, coarse hair with mixed damage levels (TE regrowth + weathered cuticle).

### 3D. Arginine — Protein Support

**Position:** 12

**Verified:** Arginine is a semi-essential amino acid and one of the primary building blocks of hair keratin. It carries a positive charge (cationic) making it substantive to hair. Per INCIDecoder: "creates a complex with AHAs that causes a 'time-release AHA effect' and reduces the irritation associated with AHAs." ([INCIDecoder](https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/arginine))

**In this formula:** Arginine works synergistically with citric acid. The positively charged arginine is attracted to damaged hair, and its interaction with citric acid (an AHA) may provide a buffered, sustained pH effect on the hair surface.

**Delivery format validation:** In a conditioner (1-2 min contact), arginine has time to deposit on the hair surface. However, at position 12 (after fragrance, isopropyl alcohol, phenoxyethanol, and benzoic acid), the concentration is low. This is a supporting ingredient, not a primary active.

### 3E. Citric Acid — pH Maintenance and Bond Support

**Position:** 13

Same mechanism as in the shampoo (see shampoo deep dive section 3D), but with a key difference: in a conditioner with 1-2 minutes contact time, the citric acid has more opportunity to interact with the hair cortex than in a rinse-off shampoo.

**Assessment:** Still primarily functioning as a pH adjuster (maintaining the acidic environment that keeps the cuticle closed), with modest secondary benefits from chelation and non-covalent protein reinforcement.

### 3F. Other Notable Ingredients

- **Isopropyl Alcohol** (position 6) — solvent, helps dissolve and distribute the silicone emulsion. Evaporates during drying. Not drying to hair at this concentration in a rinse-off product.
- **Phenoxyethanol** (position 7) — preservative. Safe, gentle, industry standard. No concerns.
- **Benzoic Acid** (position 8) — co-preservative. Works with phenoxyethanol for broad-spectrum preservation.
- **Potassium Hydroxide** (position 10) — pH adjuster (alkaline, used to balance the citric acid and achieve target pH).
- **Betaine** (position 11) — humectant/osmolyte. Helps retain moisture in the hair shaft.
- **Mica + Titanium Dioxide** — pearlescent/opacifying agents. Cosmetic only (makes the product look creamy/shimmery).
- **Carvone + Citrus Aurantium Peel Oil** — fragrance components.

### 3G. What's NOT in this formula (and why that matters)

| Absent Ingredient | Why Its Absence Is Good |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Dimethicone | Non-selective, builds up, weighs down curls |
| Dimethiconol | Same issues as dimethicone, heavier |
| Isothiazolinones (MI/MCI) | Contact sensitizers |
| DMDM Hydantoin | Formaldehyde releaser |
| Parabens | Consumer concern (though scientifically safe) |
| Sulfates | Would strip conditioning agents during use |
| Isopropyl Myristate | Comedogenic (3-5), can cause scalp issues |

The current formula is notably cleaner than the older version and cleaner than the Dove conditioners.

## 8. Practical Education Section (for in-app use)

### Why you're using this

This is your every-wash conditioner — the one that detangles, smooths, and protects without building up. Its amodimethicone seeks out the most damaged parts of your hair and deposits a thin, protective film there. The behentrimonium chloride (a long-chain conditioning agent) provides the slip you need to get a comb through thick, wet hair without snapping strands.

### How to know it's working

- Wet hair detangles easily during and after application (fingers or wide-tooth comb glide through)
- Hair feels smooth and slippery when rinsing — not squeaky, not slimy
- Dried hair has shine and reduced frizz compared to no conditioner
- You don't need to clarify frequently (the amodimethicone self-limits — no progressive heaviness)
- Curl definition holds — curls spring back rather than being weighed down
- The damaged mid-lengths and ends feel softer than the roots (selective deposition working)

### How to know it's NOT working

- Hair feels heavy, limp, or greasy after drying → you may be using too much, or your hair isn't damaged enough to need this level of conditioning (unlikely for your profile)
- Hair still tangles badly during washing → try leaving it on longer (2-3 minutes instead of 1), or apply more generously to the ends
- No difference from a cheaper conditioner → your hair may have changed (less damaged = less selective deposition benefit). This is good news.
- Scalp feels coated or itchy → you're applying too close to the roots. Focus on mid-lengths and ends.

### Why this over alternatives

- **vs. Dove conditioners:** Dove uses dimethiconol (non-selective silicone) that coats uniformly and builds up over time, requiring more frequent clarifying. This one targets damage specifically and self-limits.
- **vs. silicone-free conditioners:** For coarse, damaged hair, silicone-free conditioners often don't provide enough smoothing and frizz control. Amodimethicone gives you the silicone benefits without the buildup drawbacks.
- **vs. heavy mask-type conditioners:** Those are for weekly use. This is gentle enough for every wash while still providing substantial conditioning.

### When to reach for something else

- **Deep conditioning day** (weekly) → Use a mask or deep conditioner for extended treatment (10+ minutes with heat cap)
- **Protein treatment day** → Olaplex 3 or protein-containing treatment, then follow with this conditioner to seal
- **Post-clarifying** → This conditioner is perfect after clarifying — the stripped hair will attract more amodimethicone than usual
- **If hair feels over-conditioned** → Skip conditioner for one wash, or use a lighter one. But this is rare with amodimethicone (self-limiting).

### If you're shopping for a replacement

Key things to look for:
- **Amodimethicone** (not dimethicone or dimethiconol) as the primary silicone — ideally in the top 5 ingredients
- **Behentrimonium chloride** (C22 chain) for coarse hair — shorter-chain quats (cetrimonium) provide less slip
- **Cetearyl alcohol** high in the list — provides the body and lubrication coarse hair needs
- **No sulfates** in the conditioner (some cheap conditioners contain them — defeats the purpose)
- **Acidic pH** (citric acid or similar)

Red flags to avoid:
- Dimethicone or Dimethiconol as primary silicone (buildup)
- Isopropyl Myristate high in the list (comedogenic, can cause scalp issues)
- DMDM Hydantoin (formaldehyde releaser)
- Very short ingredient list with no silicone at all (may not provide enough smoothing for coarse hair)
