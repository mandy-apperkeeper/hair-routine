## 1. Executive Summary

The Garnier Fructis Hair Filler Strength Repair Serum is a standard silicone smoothing serum marketed under the "Vitamin Cg" brand umbrella. The functional mechanism is straightforward: isododecane carrier evaporates, depositing a dimethicone + dimethiconol film that smooths, adds shine, and reduces frizz. The headline ingredient — ascorbyl glucoside ("Vitamin Cg") at position 15 — has zero peer-reviewed evidence for hair fiber repair; all published studies are skin-focused. The "79% stronger hair" claim is for the full 4-step Hair Filler system, not this serum alone. The actual structural repair in that system comes from the pre-shampoo's citric acid crosslinking (position 6, high concentration), not this serum's trace citric acid (position 21, pH adjuster only).

**Verdict:** Tier should be downgraded to **Use-Up**. This product is outclassed by the Dove 10-in-1 Serum (aminopropyl dimethicone — selective deposition, cheaper) and the L'Oréal 21-in-1 Spray (amodimethicone + coconut oil + UV filter, cheaper). The non-selective silicones here coat all hair equally rather than targeting damage, and the price ($2.63/fl oz) is nearly double the superior alternatives.

## 3. Mechanism Assessment

### 3A. Isododecane (Position 1) — Volatile Carrier

**Verified:** Isododecane is a volatile hydrocarbon emollient/solvent that evaporates after application, leaving behind the non-volatile ingredients. [INCIDecoder]

**Role:** Same concept as cyclopentasiloxane in the Dove 10-in-1 — a fast-evaporating carrier that spreads the product evenly, then disappears. After evaporation, only the dimethicone/dimethiconol deposit remains on hair.

### 3B. Dimethicone (Position 4) + Dimethiconol (Position 6) — NON-SELECTIVE Silicone Coating

**Verified:** Dimethicone is a non-volatile silicone polymer that coats hair, providing smoothing, shine, and frizz control via a protective barrier. [INCIDecoder, Wimpole Clinic 2025]

**Verified:** Dimethiconol is a higher molecular weight silicone providing "intense smoothing, detangling, and shine. Heavier than dimethicone, it provides longer-lasting frizz control and moisture sealing." [HairAide, 2025]

**Critical distinction:** Both are NON-SELECTIVE silicones. They coat all hair equally — healthy sections and damaged sections receive the same amount of product. This is inferior to:
- **Aminopropyl dimethicone** (in Dove 10-in-1) — amine-functionalized, selectively targets negatively-charged damaged areas
- **Amodimethicone** (in L'Oréal 21-in-1) — also selectively deposits on damaged areas

For damaged hair, selective silicones are superior because they deposit more product where damage is worst, providing targeted repair rather than uniform coating.

**Buildup concern:** Both are non-volatile silicones that accumulate with repeated use. For 2C-3A curly/wavy hair, this can weigh down curls and require clarifying shampoo to remove. [Van + Veronica, 2024; Wimpole Clinic, 2025]

**Position significance:** Positions 4 and 6 = moderate-to-high concentration. These are the primary functional ingredients after the carrier evaporates.

### 3C. Ascorbyl Glucoside / "Vitamin Cg" (Position 15) — MARKETING INGREDIENT

**Verified:** Ascorbyl glucoside is a stable vitamin C derivative. Mechanism: enzymatic hydrolysis releases bioactive vitamin C providing antioxidant protection. [ChemicalBook, 2025; INCIDecoder]

**Hair-specific evidence:** NONE FOUND. All peer-reviewed studies on ascorbyl glucoside are for SKIN (antioxidant, collagen synthesis, melanin inhibition). No published research demonstrates ascorbyl glucoside repairing hair fiber structure.

**Position significance:** Position 15 of 27 = LOW concentration (likely 0.1-0.5%). Below the preservative (phenoxyethanol at position 10, max 1%). This is a marketing-level inclusion.

**Garnier's claim:** "Vitamin Cg strengthens broken bonds within the hair cortex, yielding up to 79% more strength." [Garnier.ca] — This claim is for the SYSTEM (pre-shampoo + shampoo + conditioner + serum together), not the serum alone. The strength benefit comes from the pre-shampoo's citric acid crosslinking, not the serum's vitamin C.

### 3D. Citric Acid (Position 21) — pH ADJUSTER ONLY

**Critical distinction from the pre-shampoo:** In the Garnier Hair Filler Pre-Shampoo, citric acid is at position 6 (high concentration, functional active for KAP crosslinking — peer-reviewed evidence: PubMed 39757966, Jan 2025). In this serum, it's at position 21 of 27 = TRACE amount, functioning only as a pH adjuster/buffer.

No crosslinking function at this concentration. The peer-reviewed evidence for citric acid crosslinking used concentrated solutions with extended dwell time. Position 21 in a leave-in serum is orders of magnitude below functional concentration.

### 3E. Hydroxyethyl Urea (Position 9) — Humectant

**Verified:** A urea derivative that works as a strong moisturizer/humectant. Similar moisturizing ability to glycerin but with a non-sticky, non-tacky feel. [INCIDecoder]

**Position significance:** Position 9 = moderate concentration. Provides some hydration benefit beneath the silicone layer. This is the one genuinely useful supporting ingredient in the formula.

### 3F. Alcohol Denat. (Position 5) — Solvent/Penetration Enhancer

At position 5, this is a significant amount of denatured alcohol. Aids in spreading and quick-drying but can be drying to hair with repeated use. The silicone coating may mitigate this, but it's worth noting for already-dry hair types.

### 3G. PEG-14 Dimethicone (Position 8) — Emulsifier

Helps the water-phase ingredients (glycerin, hydroxyethyl urea) mix with the silicone-phase ingredients. Functional helper, not an active.

### 3H. Arginine (Position 20) — Amino Acid (trace)

At position 20, this is a trace inclusion — likely for marketing ("repair care complex") rather than functional benefit. Same trace-level arginine found in the pre-shampoo and conditioner.

## 4. Delivery Format Validation

| Factor | Assessment | Impact |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Format | Leave-in serum (lightweight liquid) | Good distribution, fast absorption |
| Application state | Damp or dry hair after washing | Flexible application |
| Carrier system | Isododecane (volatile hydrocarbon) | Evaporates cleanly, deposits silicones |
| Active delivery | Surface coating only | Dimethicone/dimethiconol do NOT penetrate the cortex |
| Buildup risk | MODERATE — non-volatile silicones accumulate | Requires periodic clarifying |
| Rinse requirement | None (leave-in) | Convenient but contributes to buildup |

**Delivery verdict:** The format is fine for what it is — a silicone smoothing serum. The volatile carrier ensures even distribution and the leave-in format provides ongoing surface protection. However, the format does NOT support the marketing claims of "inner fiber repair" or "7 layers deep" — dimethicone and dimethiconol are surface-coating agents that cannot penetrate the cortex.

**The "79% stronger" claim** requires the full 4-step system. The pre-shampoo (citric acid at position 6, 5-10 min dwell time) does the actual structural repair. This serum's contribution is surface coating/protection only.

- **Alcohol denat at position 5:** May contribute to dryness between washes on already-dry coarse hair.

## 8. Practical Education Section (for in-app use)

### Why you're using this

This is a silicone smoothing serum — it coats your hair with a thin film of dimethicone and dimethiconol after the liquid carrier evaporates. The coating smooths the cuticle, adds shine, reduces frizz, and makes hair feel silky. The "Vitamin Cg" on the label is a marketing angle — there's no published evidence that ascorbyl glucoside repairs hair fiber. The actual smoothing comes from the silicones, same as any silicone serum.

It's part of the Garnier Hair Filler system (pre-shampoo → shampoo → conditioner → serum), but the serum's role in that system is surface protection, not structural repair. The structural repair comes from the pre-shampoo's citric acid.

### How to know it's working

- Hair feels smoother and silkier after application
- Visible shine, especially in direct light
- Reduced frizz and flyaways
- Easier detangling
- Ends look less rough/split (the coating fills in surface irregularities)

### How to know it's NOT working

- Hair feels heavy, limp, or greasy → silicone buildup. Time to clarify.
- Curls losing definition or bounce → the non-selective silicone is weighing them down. Switch to a selective silicone product (Dove 10-in-1 or L'Oréal 21-in-1).
- Products applied after this aren't absorbing → the silicone barrier is blocking them. Apply water-based products first, silicone serum last.
- No improvement in hair strength or breakage → expected. This product doesn't repair — it coats. For actual strength improvement, the pre-shampoo is the product doing that work.

### Why this over alternatives

Honestly? There isn't a strong reason to choose this over the alternatives in your inventory:
- **Dove 10-in-1** targets damage selectively and costs less
- **L'Oréal 21-in-1** adds penetrating coconut oil + UV protection and costs less
- **This serum** provides basic silicone smoothing at a higher price point

The main reason to keep using it: if you're using the full Hair Filler system and want consistency, or if you simply prefer the texture/feel of this specific serum over the alternatives.

### When to reach for something else

- **For targeted damage repair** → Dove 10-in-1 (aminopropyl dimethicone selectively deposits on damaged areas)
- **For UV protection + penetrating moisture** → L'Oréal 21-in-1 (coconut oil + UV filter + amodimethicone)
- **For heat styling days** → Marc Anthony Shield (Polysilicone-29 creates a humidity-blocking film that this serum cannot)
- **If curls are losing definition** → Switch to a selective silicone product that won't uniformly coat and weigh down healthy sections

### If you're shopping for a replacement

Key things to look for:
- **Amodimethicone or aminopropyl dimethicone** (selective silicones that target damage) over plain dimethicone/dimethiconol
- **Additional functional ingredients** (penetrating oils, UV filters, proteins) rather than marketing vitamins
- **Lower price per ounce** — basic silicone serums shouldn't cost $2.63/oz when better alternatives exist at $1.30-1.50/oz

Red flags:
- "Vitamin" or "repair" claims without specifying the mechanism → likely marketing
- System claims ("X% stronger with full system") attributed to a single product → the benefit comes from the system, not the individual product
- Non-selective silicones (dimethicone, dimethiconol) marketed as "repair" → they coat, they don't repair
