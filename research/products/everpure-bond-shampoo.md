## 1. Executive Summary

The EverPure Bond Repair Shampoo is a well-formulated sulfate-free shampoo built on a 5-surfactant mild cleansing system (Coco-Betaine, Disodium Laureth Sulfosuccinate, Sodium Cocoyl Isethionate, Sodium Lauryl Sulfoacetate, Sodium Lauroyl Sarcosinate). It deposits amodimethicone selectively onto damaged hair during washing, uses Polyquaternium-10 as a conditioning polymer for wet detangling, and maintains an acidic pH via citric acid for cuticle closure. L'Oréal's "Bond + Amino Acid Complex" marketing claim maps to citric acid (non-covalent protein network reinforcement) — a legitimate but modest mechanism in a rinse-off format. This is a strong primary-tier shampoo for damaged, coarse, curly hair.

**Verdict:** Tier confirmed — Primary Rotation. No change needed.

## 3. Mechanism Assessment

### 3A. Surfactant System — Gentle Cleansing

The shampoo uses 5 mild surfactants in combination rather than a single harsh one:

| Position | Surfactant | Type | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|------|-----------|
| 1 | Coco-Betaine | Amphoteric | Foam stabilizer, mildness booster |
| 2 | Disodium Laureth Sulfosuccinate | Anionic (mild) | Primary cleanser, very low irritation potential |
| 3 | Sodium Cocoyl Isethionate (SCI) | Anionic (mild) | "Baby foam" surfactant — micelles too large to penetrate skin barrier |
| 4 | Sodium Lauryl Sulfoacetate (SLSA) | Anionic (mild) | Gentle cleanser, rich foam |
| 5 | Sodium Lauroyl Sarcosinate | Anionic (mild) | Foam booster, improves mildness |

**Verified:** SCI's mildness is mechanistically explained by micelle size. Per Sigma-Aldrich (citing Ananthapadmanabhan et al., 2007): "In vitro and in vivo studies have demonstrated that SCI is mild and less damaging to the skin barrier than soaps and surfactants such as sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS)." The micelles are too large to penetrate the aqueous pores of the stratum corneum. ([Sigma-Aldrich/J Cosmet Sci, 2007](https://www.sigmaaldrich.com/US/en/tech-docs/paper/242670))

**Verified:** Disodium Laureth Sulfosuccinate is described by manufacturers as "very mild, non-irritating" with "great foam properties." ([INCIDecoder](https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/disodium-laureth-sulfosuccinate))

**Assessment:** This is one of the mildest surfactant systems available in a mass-market shampoo. The combination of 5 different mild surfactants at lower individual concentrations produces effective cleansing with minimal protein stripping or lipid removal. For coarse, dry, 2C-3A hair that's already prone to moisture loss, this is the correct approach.

**Comparison to Dove shampoos:** The Dove Bond Strength uses Sodium C12-13 Pareth Sulfate (ethoxylated sulfate) as primary surfactant. The Dove Intensive Repair uses SLES. Both are significantly more stripping than this sulfate-free system.

### 3B. Amodimethicone — Selective Conditioning in a Shampoo

**Position:** 13 (after fragrance and salt — low concentration, but this is expected for amodimethicone in shampoo)

**Verified:** Amodimethicone selectively binds to damaged, negatively charged areas of hair via its protonated amine groups. Per Lab Muffin (Wong, April 2024): "Opposite charges attract, so this means that amodimethicone will selectively stick to the damaged sections of hair more strongly." It also self-limits — "Any amodimethicone that's already stuck to the hair will repel additional amodimethicone" due to like-charge repulsion. ([Lab Muffin, April 2024](https://labmuffin.com/amodimethicone-my-new-favourite-hair-ingredient/))

**Verified:** Amodimethicone is delivered in an emulsion system here (PPG-5-Ceteth-20 + Trideceth-6 + Cetrimonium Chloride form the delivery system — these three ingredients together are the standard amodimethicone emulsion package). Per Cosmetics & Toiletries (2013): amodimethicone cationic emulsion is "recommended at 1-5% as a conditioning additive for hair care products such as shampoos." ([Cosmetics & Toiletries, 2013](https://www.cosmeticsandtoiletries.com/cosmetic-ingredients/moisturizing/news/21844479/amodimethicone-cationic-emulsion-for-hair-care-conditioning))

**Delivery format validation:** In a rinse-off shampoo, amodimethicone has limited contact time (~2-3 minutes). However, the cationic charge means it deposits during the dilution/rinse phase (the "complex coacervation" mechanism — as surfactant concentration drops during rinsing, the cationic silicone precipitates out of solution and deposits onto the negatively charged hair surface). This is the same mechanism that makes Polyquaternium-10 effective in shampoos.

**Assessment:** Amodimethicone in a shampoo provides less conditioning than in a conditioner (shorter contact, lower concentration), but it's still a meaningful benefit — particularly the selective deposition on damaged areas. For Mandy's weathered cuticle and TE regrowth (mixed damage levels along the strand), selective deposition is especially valuable.

### 3C. Polyquaternium-10 — Conditioning Polymer

**Position:** 11 (relatively high — above citric acid and amodimethicone)

**Verified:** PQ-10 is a cellulose-derived cationic polymer. Per HairAide (2025): "Polyquaternium-10 carries a positive electrical charge that causes it to be attracted to and deposit onto the negatively charged surface of hair, especially damaged areas. This deposition forms a thin conditioning film that reduces static, improves wet combability, and adds softness." ([HairAide, 2025](https://hairaide.com/ingredients/polyquaternium-10/))

**Verified:** Per Cosmetics & Toiletries (2013): "Cationic polymers, specifically polyquaternium-10 and guar hydroxypropyltrimonium chloride, have been included in shampoos to confer wet-conditioning benefits and style to the hair after drying." The deposition occurs via "dilution precipitation mechanism, which occurs during in-use product rinsing." ([Cosmetics & Toiletries, 2013](https://www.cosmeticsandtoiletries.com/formulas-products/hair-care/article/21833660/advances-in-polymers-for-hair-conditioning-shampoos))

**Assessment:** PQ-10 is the industry-standard conditioning polymer for shampoos. It provides immediate wet-comb improvement and reduces friction during washing — important for thick, coarse hair that tangles easily when wet. The Dove shampoos use Guar Hydroxypropyltrimonium Chloride for the same purpose — both are legitimate, with PQ-10 being slightly lighter-feeling.

### 3D. Citric Acid — The "Bond Repair" Claim

**Position:** 12

**L'Oréal's claim:** "Bond + Amino Acid Complex rebuilds broken bonds to restore strength. Tightens keratin microfibrils and strengthens hair structure." ([L'Oréal Paris USA](https://www.lorealparisusa.com/hair-care-hair-style/shampoo/sulfate-free-bond-strengthening-color-care-shampoo))

**What the science actually says:**

**Verified:** Per PMID 39757966 (Jan 2026): Citric acid treatment "demonstrably enhances the structural integrity of chemically treated hair. DSC revealed increased crosslinking density of keratin-associated proteins (KAPs) in the hair cortex, evidenced by a 6.7% to 15.0% increase in denaturation temperature." The mechanisms include "calcium chelation, the treatment's pH, and reinforcement of the non-covalent protein network." ([ResearchGate/Int J Cosmet Sci, Jan 2026](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/387757465_Reinforcing_chemically_treated_human_hair_with_citric_acid))

**Verified:** Per Cosmetics & Toiletries (2021): "Hair soaked in [carboxylic acid] solutions showed a marked increase in protein denaturation temperature — in some instances by up to 10°C." ([Cosmetics & Toiletries, 2021](https://www.cosmeticsandtoiletries.com/testing/efficacy/article/21837243/your-hair-on-acid-the-influence-of-carboxylic-acids))

**Verified:** Per HairAide (2025): "Citric Acid lowers the pH of hair care formulations to the mildly acidic range optimal for hair cuticle closure. A closed cuticle reflects more light (shine), retains moisture better, and resists damage. It also chelates calcium and magnesium ions from hard water." Effective concentration: 0.1-2%. ([HairAide, 2025](https://hairaide.com/ingredients/citric-acid/))

**Delivery format validation — CRITICAL CAVEAT:** The 2026 study (PMID 39757966) used citric acid as a *treatment* — soaking hair in citric acid solution. In a rinse-off shampoo with ~2-3 minutes contact time, the crosslinking effect is significantly reduced. The pH-mediated cuticle closure and chelation effects are more relevant in this format than the deeper cortical crosslinking.

**Assessment:** L'Oréal's "bond repair" claim is partially supported by science but overstated for a shampoo format. Citric acid in this product primarily:
1. Maintains acidic pH → cuticle closure → shine + moisture retention (legitimate, immediate)
2. Chelates hard water minerals → prevents buildup (legitimate, immediate)
3. May provide modest non-covalent protein reinforcement with repeated use (plausible but unquantified in rinse-off format)

It does NOT "rebuild broken bonds" in the way Olaplex (bis-aminopropyl diglycol dimaleate) does — that's a fundamentally different mechanism (covalent disulfide bond reformation). Citric acid reinforces the *non-covalent* protein network. This is still beneficial but is a different, weaker type of "repair."

### 3E. Additional Actives (Reformulated Version Only)

The Sept 2023 INCIDecoder version adds:

- **Arginine** (position 19) — cationic amino acid, substantive to hair, natural component of keratin. Provides mild conditioning and may support protein structure. Low concentration in rinse-off = modest benefit.
- **2-Oleamido-1,3-Octadecanediol** (position 34) — a synthetic pseudo-ceramide. Per Moumoujus: "mimics the natural lipid architecture" and "integrates into the intercellular matrix." ([Moumoujus](https://moumoujus.com/ingredients/2-oleamido-1-3-octadecanediol/)) In hair context: ceramides fill gaps in the cuticle lipid layer (the cell membrane complex). However, at position 34 in a rinse-off product, the concentration is vanishingly small. Marketing ingredient at this level.
- **Polyquaternium-7** (position 17) — additional conditioning polymer, works synergistically with PQ-10.
- **Tocopherol** (Vitamin E) — antioxidant, protects formula stability.
- **Sunflower Seed Oil** (position 35) — emollient, trace amount.
- **Rosemary Leaf Extract** (position 37) — antioxidant, trace amount.

**Assessment:** The reformulated version is marginally better (additional conditioning polymers, pseudo-ceramide), but the core mechanism is identical. The added ingredients are at concentrations too low to meaningfully change performance in a rinse-off format.

## 8. Practical Education Section (for in-app use)

### Why you're using this

This is your everyday shampoo — the one that cleans without stripping. Its 5-surfactant system is designed to remove dirt and excess oil while leaving your hair's natural moisture and any beneficial product residue (like amodimethicone from your conditioner) largely intact. It also deposits a small amount of its own amodimethicone during rinsing, targeting the most damaged sections of your hair.

### How to know it's working

- Hair feels clean but not squeaky after washing (squeaky = over-stripped)
- Wet hair has some slip — you can run fingers through without major snagging
- Hair dries with more shine than with a sulfate shampoo
- You don't need to clarify as often (the amodimethicone self-limits, so buildup is minimal)
- Curl definition holds better than with sulfate shampoos (less cuticle disruption = less frizz)

### How to know it's NOT working

- Hair feels limp or coated after washing → unlikely with this formula, but if it happens, you may be over-conditioning elsewhere in the routine
- Scalp feels itchy or has residue → the mild surfactants may not be removing heavy styling products. Use clarifying shampoo periodically.
- No difference from a cheaper sulfate shampoo → your hair may not be damaged enough to benefit from the selective conditioning. (This is good news — it means your hair is healthy.)

### Why this over alternatives

- **vs. Dove shampoos:** Dove uses sulfates that strip more moisture from coarse/curly hair, and deposits non-selective silicones that build up and require more frequent clarifying.
- **vs. other sulfate-free shampoos:** Many sulfate-free shampoos use only 1-2 mild surfactants and struggle to actually clean thick hair. This one uses 5 in combination — effective cleansing without harshness.
- **vs. "bond repair" shampoos with actual bond-reforming agents:** Those don't exist in shampoo format at mass-market price points. The citric acid here provides real (if modest) structural support.

### When to reach for something else

- **Heavy product buildup** (silicone-heavy styling, hard water deposits) → Clarifying shampoo (Kinky Curly Come Clean or EverPure Clarifying)
- **Scalp issues** (dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis) → Medicated shampoo as needed, then return to this
- **Post-swimming** (chlorine/salt) → Chelating/clarifying wash first

### If you're shopping for a replacement

Key things to look for:
- **Sulfate-free** — look for Sodium Cocoyl Isethionate, Coco-Betaine, Disodium Laureth Sulfosuccinate, or Sodium Lauroyl Sarcosinate as primary surfactants
- **Amodimethicone** (not dimethicone or dimethiconol) — selective conditioning without buildup
- **Acidic pH** (citric acid or similar) — cuticle closure
- **Conditioning polymer** (PQ-10 or Guar HPTC) — wet detangling

Red flags to avoid:
- Sodium Lauryl Sulfate or Sodium Laureth Sulfate as primary surfactant
- Dimethicone or Dimethiconol high in the ingredient list (buildup risk)
- DMDM Hydantoin (formaldehyde releaser)
