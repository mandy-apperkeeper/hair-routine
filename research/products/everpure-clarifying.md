## 1. Executive Summary

The EverPure Clarifying Shampoo is a sulfate-free clarifying shampoo that uses Sodium C14-16 Olefin Sulfonate (AOS) as its primary surfactant — a stronger cleanser than the Bond Repair shampoo's SCI/sulfosuccinate system, but still technically sulfate-free. It's designed for periodic use to remove silicone buildup, hard water mineral deposits, and styling product residue. The formula is notably stripped-down (only 20 ingredients), contains NO silicones, NO conditioning polymers beyond PQ-67, and includes glycine (amino acid) + ascorbic acid (vitamin C) as antioxidants. It also includes salicylic acid for scalp exfoliation.

This is a purpose-built tool: use it when buildup accumulates, then return to the gentler Bond Repair shampoo for daily washing.

**Verdict:** Tier confirmed — Primary Rotation (periodic use tool, not daily driver).

## 3. Mechanism Assessment

### 3A. Surfactant System — Stronger Cleansing for Buildup Removal

| Position | Surfactant | Type | Strength | Role |
|----------|-----------|------|----------|------|
| 2 | Sodium C14-16 Olefin Sulfonate (AOS) | Anionic (moderate-strong) | Stronger than SCI/sulfosuccinate | Primary cleanser — removes silicone/mineral buildup |
| 3 | Cocamidopropyl Betaine | Amphoteric (mild) | Mild | Foam stabilizer, mildness buffer |
| 4 | Laureth-5 Carboxylic Acid | Anionic (mild) | Mild-moderate | Secondary cleanser, helps dissolve oily residue |
| 9 | Caprylyl/Capryl Glucoside | Non-ionic (very mild) | Very mild | Gentle co-surfactant, foam booster |

**Verified:** Per Rustic Strength (2025): Sodium C14-16 Olefin Sulfonate "is in the olefin sulfonate class of chemicals, not to be confused with sulfates like SLS or SLES." It's "one of the mildest surfactants on the market" with "low irritation and ecotoxicity" and is "highly biodegradable." On the EPA's Safer Choice list. ([Rustic Strength, 2025](https://rusticstrength.com/blogs/ingredients/what-is-sodium-c14-16-alpha-olefin-sulfonate-aos-40-and-what-does-it-do))

**Verified:** Per CGProducten (2025): AOS "resembles milder variants in name, but in practice, it's just as strong as classic sulfates. It's often used in clarifying shampoos." ([CGProducten, 2025](https://www.cgproducten.nl/en/blogs/information-about-ingredients/sulfaten-in-shampoo-goed-om-te-weten-voor-gezond-haar))

**Verified:** Per Biology Insights (2025): AOS has "effective detergency and ability to produce a rich, stable lather, even in hard water conditions." ([Biology Insights, 2025](https://biologyinsights.com/what-is-sodium-c14-16-olefin-sulfonate/))

**Verified:** Per Cosmetics & Toiletries (2022): "Lower molecular weight grades [C14-16] are more soluble and produce better foam, while those with higher molecular weights have increased detergency." ([Cosmetics & Toiletries, 2022](https://www.cosmeticsandtoiletries.com/cosmetic-ingredients/cleansing/article/21837056/examining-tomorrows-surfactant-personalities-alpha-olefin-sulfonate-in-personal-care))

**Assessment — nuanced:** AOS is technically sulfate-free (it's a sulfonate, not a sulfate — different chemistry). However, its cleansing strength is comparable to SLES. This is intentional for a clarifying shampoo — you NEED stronger cleansing to remove silicone buildup and mineral deposits. The key difference from SLS/SLES: AOS is less irritating to the scalp despite similar cleansing power, and it's biodegradable.

**Comparison to Bond Repair shampoo:** The Bond Repair uses 5 very mild surfactants (SCI, sulfosuccinate, sarcosinate, coco-betaine, SLSA). The Clarifying uses 1 strong surfactant (AOS) buffered by 3 milder ones. The Clarifying will strip more — that's the point. It removes what the gentle daily shampoo leaves behind.

### 3B. Laureth-5 Carboxylic Acid — Ether Carboxylic Acid Surfactant (Position 4)

This is a less common surfactant that deserves attention. Ether carboxylic acids are anionic surfactants that are particularly effective at dissolving oily/waxy residues (like silicone buildup) while being milder than traditional sulfates. They work well in hard water and at acidic pH — both relevant for a clarifying shampoo.

**Assessment:** Smart formulation choice. Laureth-5 Carboxylic Acid complements the AOS by targeting oily/waxy buildup specifically, while AOS handles water-soluble residue and mineral deposits.

### 3C. Glycine — Amino Acid (Position 8)

**Verified:** Glycine is a non-essential amino acid and a building block of keratin. Per INCIDecoder: it "works as a moisturizer and maybe more" — may help with tissue repair. ([INCIDecoder](https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/glycine))

**In this formula:** At position 8 (above fragrance), glycine is at meaningful concentration. In a clarifying shampoo that strips more aggressively, glycine serves as a compensating ingredient — it deposits on the hair during washing to partially offset the stripping effect. Amino acids are substantive to hair (they integrate into the protein structure).

**Assessment:** Thoughtful formulation. The glycine partially compensates for the stronger cleansing — you get the buildup removal without leaving hair completely stripped of everything.

### 3D. Salicylic Acid — Scalp Exfoliation (Position 15)

**Verified:** Salicylic acid is a BHA (beta-hydroxy acid) that's oil-soluble, meaning it can penetrate into pores and dissolve sebum plugs. Per INCIDecoder: "It can exfoliate skin both on the surface and in the pores" and is "a potent anti-inflammatory agent." ([INCIDecoder](https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/salicylic-acid))

**In this formula:** At position 15 (low concentration, likely <0.5%), salicylic acid provides mild scalp exfoliation — helping to remove dead skin cells, sebum buildup, and product residue from the scalp surface. This is particularly relevant for people who use heavy styling products or have hard water.

**Assessment:** A genuine functional ingredient at this position, not just a preservative (though it also serves that role). It addresses scalp health specifically — something the Bond Repair shampoo doesn't target.

### 3E. Polyquaternium-67 — Conditioning Polymer (Position 16)

PQ-67 is a silicone-containing conditioning polymer (a polyquaternium with dimethicone grafted onto it). It provides mild conditioning during washing without leaving heavy silicone residue — it's designed to deposit a very thin, lightweight conditioning film.

**Assessment:** This is the formula's concession to not leaving hair completely stripped. PQ-67 provides just enough conditioning to prevent the "straw-like" feel that pure clarifying shampoos can cause, without defeating the purpose of clarifying.

### 3F. Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C) — Antioxidant (Position 20)

At the last position, ascorbic acid is at very low concentration. Its primary role here is likely:
1. Antioxidant protection for the formula itself (prevents oxidation)
2. Chelation assistance — vitamin C can help break down chlorine and mineral deposits
3. Marketing ("with Antioxidants" claim)

**Assessment:** Minimal functional benefit for hair at this concentration in a rinse-off product. Primarily a formula stabilizer and marketing ingredient.

### 3G. Citric Acid — Chelation + pH (Position 12)

Same as in the Bond Repair shampoo, but here the chelation function is more important. Citric acid binds to calcium and magnesium ions from hard water, helping to dissolve mineral deposits on the hair. In a clarifying shampoo specifically designed for hard water exposure, this is a primary functional ingredient, not just a pH adjuster.

## 8. Practical Education Section (for in-app use)

### Why you're using this

Think of this as a reset button. Your daily shampoo (Bond Repair) is gentle — it cleans without stripping. But over time, tiny amounts of silicone, mineral deposits from water, and styling product residue accumulate on your hair. This buildup makes your hair feel heavy, look dull, and prevents your conditioner from working properly. The clarifying shampoo removes all of that, giving your hair a fresh start.

### How to know it's working

- Hair feels "squeaky clean" after rinsing (this is normal and expected — it means buildup is gone)
- Hair feels lighter and bouncier after drying
- Your conditioner feels more effective the next time you use it (better absorption)
- Curl definition improves on subsequent washes
- Hair looks shinier (mineral deposits that dulled it are gone)

### How to know it's NOT working

- Hair still feels coated or heavy after use → you may need to lather twice, or the buildup is from a water-insoluble source that needs a different approach
- Scalp still feels flaky or itchy → the salicylic acid may not be enough for your scalp concern. Consider a dedicated scalp treatment.
- Hair feels extremely dry/straw-like after → you may be clarifying too often. Reduce frequency and always follow with conditioner.

### Why this over alternatives

- **vs. Kinky Curly Come Clean:** Kinky Curly is very gentle — great for light maintenance but may not remove heavy silicone buildup. This one is stronger.
- **vs. sulfate clarifying shampoos (Neutrogena, etc.):** Those use SLES which is more irritating to the scalp. This achieves similar cleansing with less irritation.
- **vs. apple cider vinegar rinses:** ACV is good for mineral removal but doesn't dissolve silicone buildup. This does both.
- **vs. baking soda:** Don't. Baking soda is alkaline (pH ~9) and damages the cuticle. This is properly pH-balanced.

### When to reach for something else

- **Daily washing** → Use Bond Repair shampoo (this is too strong for every day)
- **Dandruff/seborrheic dermatitis** → Use a medicated shampoo (zinc pyrithione, ketoconazole)
- **Chlorine removal specifically** → A vitamin C spray before swimming is more effective than clarifying after
- **If this feels too harsh** → Try using it only on the scalp and letting the lather run through lengths during rinsing

### If you're shopping for a replacement

Key things to look for:
- **Sodium C14-16 Olefin Sulfonate** or similar strong-but-sulfate-free surfactant
- **Citric acid** for mineral chelation
- **No silicones** in the clarifying shampoo itself (defeats the purpose)
- **Some conditioning compensation** (amino acids, mild conditioning polymer) to prevent over-stripping
- **Acidic pH** (protects cuticle even during strong cleansing)

Red flags to avoid:
- SLS as primary surfactant (unnecessarily harsh for periodic use)
- Silicones in a clarifying shampoo (contradictory)
- No chelating agent (won't address hard water)
- Very high pH (alkaline = cuticle damage)

## 10. Self-Critique

### What this analysis does well:
- Clearly distinguishes the role of this product (periodic tool) from the daily shampoo
- Explains the AOS sulfonate-vs-sulfate distinction honestly (technically sulfate-free, practically strong)
- Identifies the compensation strategy (glycine + PQ-67) that prevents over-stripping

### Limitations:
- Could not find peer-reviewed comparative data on AOS vs SCI cleansing strength specifically for silicone removal — the "stronger" claim is inferred from surfactant class properties and industry consensus, not a direct comparison study
- PQ-67 is not well-documented in publicly available sources — its silicone-grafted structure is inferred from its classification as a "silicone quaternium" polymer
- The "every 2-4 weeks" frequency recommendation is based on general curly hair guidance, not specific to this product or this hair profile
- Ascorbic acid's chelation-assist role at position 20 is speculative — the concentration is likely too low to meaningfully contribute

### What would change this assessment:
- If Mandy finds this too stripping even with conditioner follow-up → could switch to Kinky Curly Come Clean for lighter clarifying, reserving this for heavy buildup only
- If hard water is not an issue in Mandy's area → the chelation benefit becomes less relevant (but silicone removal still matters)
- If Mandy stops using products with dimethicone/dimethiconol → clarifying frequency could decrease significantly (amodimethicone self-limits and doesn't build up the same way)
