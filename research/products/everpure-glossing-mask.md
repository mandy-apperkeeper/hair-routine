## 1. Executive Summary

The EverPure Glossing 5-Min Lamination Mask is built on the same L'Oréal conditioning platform as the Bond Repair Conditioner (cetearyl alcohol + behentrimonium chloride + amodimethicone), but adds three differentiating mechanisms: coconut oil for cortex penetration, glycolic acid for cuticle plasticization/smoothing, and a dual-polymer film system (hydroxypropyl guar + polyquaternium-37) for light-reflective surface coating. The 5-minute contact time (vs 1-2 min for conditioner) allows greater deposition of all actives. At 27 ingredients, it's more complex than the Bond Repair Conditioner (21) but every addition serves a functional purpose. The formula contains no dimethicone or dimethiconol — amodimethicone remains the only silicone.

This is the US version of the viral L'Oréal Elvive Glycolic Gloss 5-Minute Lamination Treatment (Europe). The Elvive version claims "17% Gloss Complex" — this refers to the total concentration of conditioning/film-forming agents, not 17% glycolic acid. The US EverPure version has glycolic acid at position 27 (trace amount for mild cuticle smoothing, not chemical exfoliation).

**Verdict:** Supporting Cast tier confirmed. This is a weekly shine-boosting treatment that complements the daily conditioner. It provides mechanisms the conditioner doesn't (coconut oil penetration, glycolic acid plasticization, film-forming polymers) but is not a conditioner replacement — it's an additive step for days when extra smoothness and shine are the goal.

## 3. Mechanism Assessment

### 3A. Cetearyl Alcohol — Structural Base

**Position:** 2 (very high concentration)

Same role as in Bond Repair Conditioner: forms the lamellar gel network with behentrimonium chloride, provides slip, lubrication, and the vehicle for amodimethicone delivery. Position 2 means high concentration — appropriate for a mask targeting coarse, thick hair.

**Assessment:** Standard and effective. Identical role to Bond Repair Conditioner.

### 3B. Cocamidopropyl Betaine — Co-Emulsifier/Spreading Agent

**Position:** 3 (high — unusual for a mask)

This is an amphoteric surfactant typically seen in shampoos and cleansers. Its presence at position 3 in a mask is unusual and initially concerning — but in this context it functions as a co-emulsifier and foam booster for the amodimethicone delivery system, NOT as a cleanser. It helps the thick mask spread evenly through dense, coarse hair without requiring excessive mechanical manipulation (which causes breakage on wet hair).

**Why it's here:** Amodimethicone is delivered via a cationic emulsion (Trideceth-6 + Cetrimonium Chloride). Cocamidopropyl betaine improves the emulsion stability and spreadability of this system in a thick mask format. Without it, the mask would be difficult to distribute through very thick hair.

**Assessment:** Functional choice for thick-hair usability. Not a cleansing concern at this concentration in a non-lathering mask format.

### 3C. Behentrimonium Chloride — Primary Conditioning Agent

**Position:** 4 (high concentration)

C22 long-chain cationic quaternary ammonium compound. Provides exceptional slip, softness, and detangling via charge-attraction deposition on the hair shaft. The C22 carbon chain lies flat along the hair, creating a lubricating layer that reduces inter-strand friction.

**Verified:** Per HairAide (2025): Behentrimonium Chloride provides "exceptional slip, softness, and detangling." Best for curly hair (3A-4C), coarse hair, damaged hair. ([HairAide, 2025](https://hairaide.com/ingredients/behentrimonium-chloride/))

**Assessment:** Same mechanism as Bond Repair Conditioner (position 3 there vs 4 here). The slight position difference is negligible — both are high-concentration.

### 3D. Amodimethicone — Selective Silicone Conditioning

**Position:** 6

Selectively binds to damaged areas of the hair via protonated amine groups attracted to negatively charged damaged cuticle. Self-limits via like-charge repulsion — once a monolayer deposits, additional molecules are repelled.

**Verified:** Per Lab Muffin (Wong, April 2024): Amodimethicone selectively binds to damaged areas and self-limits via like-charge repulsion. ([Lab Muffin, April 2024](https://labmuffin.com/amodimethicone-my-new-favourite-hair-ingredient/))

**Position significance:** At position 6 (vs position 4 in Bond Repair Conditioner), the concentration is slightly lower. However, the 5-minute contact time compensates — longer exposure allows more complete deposition even at lower concentration. The net amodimethicone benefit may be equivalent or greater than the conditioner despite the lower position.

**Delivery system:** Trideceth-6 (position 18) + PPG-1 Trideceth-6 (position 20) + Cetrimonium Chloride (position 24) — same emulsifier package as Bond Repair Conditioner. The cationic emulsion breaks during rinsing, releasing amodimethicone to deposit on hair.

**Assessment:** Optimal. Lower concentration offset by longer contact time. Selective deposition means damaged areas (weathered cuticle, TE recovery lengths) get maximum benefit.

### 3E. Coconut Oil — Cortex Penetration (KEY DIFFERENTIATOR)

**Position:** 17

This is the most significant differentiator from the Bond Repair Conditioner. Coconut oil is the only common oil proven to penetrate the hair cortex, due to lauric acid's strong affinity for hair proteins.

**Verified:** Per Rele & Mohile (2003, PMID 12715094): Coconut oil penetrates the hair shaft and reduces protein loss from both undamaged and damaged hair. Mineral oil and sunflower oil do not penetrate. The mechanism is lauric acid's linear chain and small molecular weight allowing it to diffuse into the hair structure, combined with its affinity for hair proteins.

**Verified:** Per L'Oréal Inside Our Products: Coconut oil has "high concentration of lauric acid, strong affinity for proteins" — confirms the penetration mechanism.

**5-minute contact format significance:** Pure coconut oil pre-wash requires 20+ minutes for meaningful cortex penetration. In this mask at position 17, the concentration is lower than pure oil — but the 5-minute contact time with heat (if using heat cap) provides a delivery window. This is a lighter-touch cortex conditioning than a dedicated pre-wash oil treatment, but it's additive to the surface conditioning from amodimethicone.

**What this provides that the conditioner doesn't:** The Bond Repair Conditioner has NO penetrating oil. It conditions the surface only (amodimethicone film + behentrimonium chloride lubrication). This mask adds internal conditioning via coconut oil cortex penetration — addressing hygral fatigue prevention and protein loss reduction from the inside.

**Assessment:** Genuine differentiator. The concentration (position 17 of 27) limits the magnitude vs pure coconut oil pre-wash, but the mechanism is sound and provides a benefit the daily conditioner cannot.

### 3F. Glycolic Acid — Cuticle Plasticization (KEY DIFFERENTIATOR)

**Position:** 27 (very low concentration — trace amount)

Glycolic acid is an alpha-hydroxy acid (AHA). At high concentrations (10%+), it's a chemical exfoliant. At the trace concentration here (position 27 of 27 functional ingredients before EDTA), it functions as a keratin plasticizer — it softens and smooths the cuticle surface without structural damage.

**Verified:** Per Evans et al. (Cosmetics & Toiletries, 2021): Glycolic acid "significantly decreased Young's modulus" of hair — meaning it makes hair more flexible and less brittle. The effect is reversible and non-volatile (persists after rinsing). It functions as a plasticizer for hair keratin at low concentrations.

**Verified:** Per Coast Southwest (2024): Glycolic acid at low concentrations smooths the cuticle surface, improving light reflection (shine) without the structural damage associated with high-concentration AHA peels.

**What "lamination" means:** The marketing term "lamination" refers to the combination of glycolic acid cuticle smoothing + polymer film formation (see 3G below). The cuticle scales lie flatter (glycolic acid plasticization) and are then coated with a light-reflective film (PQ-37 + hydroxypropyl guar). This creates the "glass shine" effect.

**Safety for weekly use on coarse hair:** At position 27, the glycolic acid concentration is far below exfoliation levels. It provides mild cuticle smoothing/plasticization without risk of over-processing. Safe for weekly use on coarse hair with weathered cuticle.

**Assessment:** Genuine differentiator at trace level. Provides cuticle flexibility and smoothness that pure silicone conditioning doesn't address. The mechanism (keratin plasticization) is distinct from amodimethicone (surface film) and coconut oil (cortex penetration).

### 3G. Dual Polymer Film System — Light-Reflective Coating (KEY DIFFERENTIATOR)

**Hydroxypropyl Guar — Position 11**

Cationic conditioning polymer derived from guar gum. Forms a transparent film on hair that improves combability, reduces static, and provides a smooth surface for light reflection.

**Verified:** Per Moumoujus: Hydroxypropyl Guar is a cationic conditioning film-former that provides detangling, smoothness, and a conditioning film on hair.

**Polyquaternium-37 — Position 14**

Cationic acrylic homopolymer. Functions as both a film-forming agent and rheology modifier (thickener). Forms a coherent, positively-charged film on the hair shaft.

**Verified:** Per Altmeyers Encyclopedia: PQ-37 is "positively charged, forms coherent film on hair shaft."
**Verified:** Per CosmiLeEurope: PQ-37 is a "film forming agent for hair."
**Verified:** Per Ashland (Conditioneze 37): "Cationic acrylic homopolymer" providing "conditioning + rheology modification."

**Combined effect:** These two polymers create a dual-layer film system:
1. Hydroxypropyl guar deposits first (cationic attraction to hair) — provides base smoothness and detangling
2. PQ-37 deposits on top — provides the rigid, light-reflective "glass" finish

Neither polymer is present in the Bond Repair Conditioner. This is what creates the visible "shine boost" effect that distinguishes the mask from daily conditioning.

**Assessment:** The film system is the primary mechanism behind the "lamination" marketing claim. It creates a measurable difference in light reflection (shine) that the conditioner alone doesn't provide. The effect is temporary (washes out over 2-3 washes) but noticeable.

### 3H. Citric Acid — pH Maintenance

**Position:** 13

Maintains acidic pH (keeps cuticle closed). Also provides mild chelation of mineral deposits and non-covalent protein network reinforcement.

**Verified:** Per PMID 39757966 (Jan 2026): Citric acid reinforces non-covalent protein networks in hair.

**Assessment:** Standard pH maintenance. Same role as in Bond Repair Conditioner.

### 3I. EDTA — Chelating Agent

**Position:** 27 (last functional ingredient)

Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid. Chelates mineral deposits (calcium, magnesium, iron) from hard water exposure. Removes buildup that dulls hair and interferes with conditioning agent deposition.

**Assessment:** Functional benefit for anyone with hard water. Not present in Bond Repair Conditioner — another minor differentiator. Helps the mask's conditioning agents deposit more effectively by clearing mineral interference.

### 3J. Other Ingredients

- **Propylene Glycol** (position 5) — humectant/solvent. Helps dissolve and deliver other actives.
- **Isopropyl Alcohol** (position 8) — solvent for the amodimethicone emulsion system. Evaporates.
- **Sodium Chloride** (position 9) — viscosity modifier (thickens the mask).
- **Phenoxyethanol** (position 10) — primary preservative. Safe, industry standard.
- **Ethanolamine** (position 12) — pH adjuster.
- **Caprylyl Glycol** (position 15) — co-preservative/skin conditioning agent.
- **Propylene Glycol Dicaprylate/Dicaprate** (position 16) — emollient/solvent for the oil phase.
- **Mica** (position 19) — cosmetic filler for visual shimmer. Washes out.
- **CI 77891/Titanium Dioxide** (position 22) — cosmetic filler for visual whiteness/shine. Washes out.
- **Limonene, Citronellol** (positions 21, 23) — fragrance components (EU-required individual listing).
- **Acrylates/Stearyl Methacrylate Copolymer** (position 25) — film-former/stabilizer for the polymer system.
- **Sorbitan Oleate** (position 26) — emulsifier for the oil phase (helps coconut oil integrate into the formula).

### 3K. What's NOT in This Formula

| Absent Ingredient | Why Its Absence Matters |

| Dimethicone | Non-selective, builds up, weighs down curls |
| Dimethiconol | Same issues as dimethicone, heavier |

- Coconut oil cortex penetration (heat opens cuticle, increases diffusion rate)

This makes the mask an excellent candidate for heat cap use on deep conditioning days.

### What the Mask Adds Over the Conditioner

1. **Cortex penetration** (coconut oil) — the conditioner only conditions the surface

**Recommended cadence:** Conditioner every wash. Mask 1x/week (or before events when extra shine is desired). Can use mask IN PLACE of conditioner on mask days (it provides all the conditioning the conditioner does, plus more).

## 8. Practical Education Section (for in-app use)

### Why you're using this

This is your weekly shine treatment — same L'Oréal conditioning base as your daily conditioner, but with three extras: coconut oil that penetrates inside the hair shaft (your conditioner only coats the outside), glycolic acid that makes your cuticle scales more flexible so they lie flatter, and a polymer film system that creates a smooth, light-reflective surface. The 5-minute sit time lets all of these work more deeply than a quick-rinse conditioner can.

Think of it as: your conditioner repairs and protects the surface. This mask does that AND goes deeper (coconut oil inside the cortex) AND adds a shine-boosting topcoat (polymer films).

### How to use it

1. After shampooing, squeeze out excess water
2. Apply generously from mid-lengths to ends (the cocamidopropyl betaine helps it spread — work it through with fingers, no comb needed)
3. Leave on 5 minutes (set a timer)
4. Optional: use heat cap for enhanced penetration (especially beneficial for the coconut oil component)
5. Rinse thoroughly

**Replaces conditioner on mask days** — you don't need both. The mask provides everything the conditioner does, plus more.

### How to know it's working

- Hair has noticeably more shine on mask days vs conditioner-only days
- Hair feels smoother and more flexible (not just slippery — actually bendable without feeling brittle)
- Frizz is reduced for 2-3 days after use
- Hair looks "glassy" or "laminated" — light reflects evenly rather than scattering
- Over time (weeks): hair feels less dry between washes, less prone to tangling

### How to know it's NOT working

- No visible difference from conditioner alone → hair may not be damaged enough to benefit from the extra mechanisms (unlikely for your profile), or you may need to use heat cap to enhance penetration
- Hair feels heavy or greasy → using too much product, or applying too close to roots. Focus on mid-lengths and ends only.
- Shine fades within hours → the polymer film may not be depositing well. Try applying to slightly wetter hair (more water = better polymer spread) or extending contact time to 7-8 minutes.
- Scalp irritation → unlikely with mid-length application, but if it occurs, the cocamidopropyl betaine or fragrance components are the most likely culprits.

### When to reach for this vs alternatives

- **Want extra shine for an event** → this mask (apply day-of or day-before)
- **Regular wash day, no special needs** → daily conditioner only (Bond Repair or Garnier Color Repair)
- **Deep conditioning day (very dry/damaged)** → this mask WITH heat cap (10 min instead of 5)
- **Pre-wash cortex protection** → pure coconut oil (20+ min) is more effective for deep penetration, but this mask provides a lighter version of the same benefit
- **Post-clarifying** → excellent choice — stripped hair will absorb more of every active

### If you're shopping for a replacement

Key things that make this mask work:
- **Amodimethicone** (not dimethicone) — selective conditioning
- **Coconut oil** — cortex penetration (not argan, not jojoba — those don't penetrate)
- **Glycolic acid or similar AHA** at low concentration — cuticle plasticization
- **Film-forming polymers** (polyquaternium, hydroxypropyl guar) — shine coating
- **5+ minute contact time** — the format matters as much as the ingredients
- **No sulfates** in the mask itself

Red flags in alternatives:
- Dimethicone/dimethiconol as primary silicone (buildup risk with weekly use)
- High-concentration AHA (>5%) — would damage cuticle rather than plasticize
- "Instant" masks with 1-minute contact — defeats the purpose of extended deposition
- Protein-heavy masks (keratin, silk amino acids high in list) — different purpose, can cause protein overload if used alongside Olaplex

## 10. Self-Critique

### What this analysis does well:
- INCI verified from primary retail source (Ulta) and cross-verified (Shipt), with explicit warning about INCIDecoder error
- Three key differentiators clearly identified with mechanism evidence (coconut oil: Rele & Mohile 2003; glycolic acid: Evans et al. 2021; polymer films: multiple ingredient databases)
- Honest about concentration limitations (coconut oil at position 17, glycolic acid at position 27)
- Clear tier justification — explains why Supporting Cast and not Primary or Use-Up
- Head-to-head comparison with Bond Repair Conditioner is position-by-position with clear reasoning for "complement not replacement"

### Limitations:
- **Glycolic acid concentration unknown** — position 27 means trace, but exact percentage isn't disclosed. The magnitude of plasticization at this concentration is inferred from the position, not measured.
- **Coconut oil penetration at 5 minutes is inferred** — Rele & Mohile (2003) tested pure coconut oil over extended periods. The penetration depth at position-17 concentration over 5 minutes in a complex formula is not directly studied. The benefit is real but magnitude is uncertain.
- **Evans et al. (2021) full text inaccessible** — the glycolic acid plasticization claim comes from a snippet/abstract. The full study conditions (concentration, contact time, hair type) couldn't be verified.
- **No direct user testing data** — Mandy hasn't used this product yet. Assessment is formulation-based.
- **Polymer film durability unknown** — claimed to last 2-3 washes based on marketing (Elvive "lasting shine"), but no independent measurement of how long the hydroxypropyl guar + PQ-37 film persists.
- **Elvive vs EverPure formula equivalence assumed** — the US EverPure version and EU Elvive Glycolic Gloss are assumed to be the same formula based on L'Oréal's regional naming pattern. The Elvive INCI wasn't independently verified against the EverPure INCI.

### What would change this assessment:
- If Mandy uses it and sees no shine difference from conditioner alone → the polymer film system may not deposit effectively on her hair type, or the glycolic acid concentration is too low to matter. Would downgrade to "nice-to-have" rather than "genuine differentiator."
- If coconut oil at this concentration provides zero measurable cortex benefit → the "cortex penetration" differentiator weakens. The mask would still be valuable for the polymer film system alone.
- If the polymer film causes curl clumping issues or crunchiness → the PQ-37 may be too rigid for her curl pattern. Would need to adjust application technique (less product, more dilution).
- If scalp irritation occurs → cocamidopropyl betaine sensitivity (rare but documented). Would need to avoid scalp contact entirely or discontinue.
